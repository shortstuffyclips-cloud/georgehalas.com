Founding the Bears

From Decatur to Chicago: building a franchise that could survive

The Chicago Bears did not begin as a powerhouse franchise. They began as a company-sponsored team in central Illinois, operating in a professional football world that lacked stability, structure, and long-term vision. George Halas’s decisions during these formative years would determine not only the Bears’ future, but the future of the NFL itself.

The Decatur Staleys and early pro football

In the late 1910s, professional football teams were often sponsored by factories or local businesses. The Decatur Staleys were backed by the A. E. Staley Manufacturing Company and operated with modest resources, limited attendance, and uncertain schedules.

Halas joined the Staleys as a player and organizer, quickly becoming the team’s driving force. Even at this early stage, he recognized that small-town football had hard limits. Without larger crowds and media attention, the sport would never gain legitimacy.

1920: organizing a league instead of chaos

In 1920, representatives from professional teams gathered in Canton, Ohio, to establish order in the sport. Halas attended as the Staleys’ representative. That meeting led to the formation of the American Professional Football Association, later renamed the NFL.

At the time, teams frequently folded midseason, rules varied by region, and championships were disputed. Halas supported the idea that professional football needed centralized governance and shared standards to survive.

The Chicago move: survival through scale

In 1921, Halas moved the Staleys from Decatur to Chicago. The decision was driven by survival, not prestige. Chicago offered a major sports market, newspaper coverage, and established venues like Cubs Park.

Financial backing from A. E. Staley helped fund the move, with the condition that the team retain the “Staleys” name for one season. After that, Halas rebranded the team as the Chicago Bears.

Branding the Bears

The name “Bears” aligned the football club with the Chicago Cubs, reinforcing the idea that professional football belonged alongside baseball as a major-league sport. This early branding decision helped fans identify with the team and its city.

It was one of the first examples of intentional sports branding in professional football — a reminder that Halas understood marketing as well as competition.

The Red Grange effect

In 1925, the Bears signed Red Grange, the most famous football player in America. Attendance surged immediately, and the Bears became the center of professional football’s first national spotlight.

A grueling barnstorming tour followed, exposing professional football to audiences across the country. While exhausting, the tour proved that pro football could draw crowds and generate revenue nationwide.

From experiment to institution

By the late 1920s, the Bears were no longer a fragile experiment. They had a home, a brand, and a central role in a league that was slowly stabilizing. Halas’s insistence on professionalism helped turn chaos into continuity.

The Bears’ rise mirrored the NFL’s rise — from uncertainty to legitimacy — and the Chicago move remains one of the most important decisions in league history.

Why it mattered

The move from Decatur to Chicago was more than a relocation. It was one of the first moments when professional football acted like a long-term business rather than a local novelty.
